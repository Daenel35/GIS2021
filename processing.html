THE MODEL INPUT

DATA PREPROCESSING FOR SUSCEPTIBILITY MAPPING

Most landslide-prone regions on earth are mountainous, composed by coarse soil and/or lack vegetation which helps anchoring the soil with their roots, or have high moisture content due to slope orientation or proximity to water bodies. All these key elements are environmental factors that can be used to model and estimate landslide susceptibility for a given area. Landslide inventories from competent authorities are also useful for indentifying areas that have already been affected by this natural phenomena. A model for landslide susceptibility mapping and exposure assessment has been implemented for the Tirano region in Lombardy, northern Italy, in a series of steps described in this section.

All of the raster input layers for environmental factors were clipped by mask according to the Tirano area, and resampled when necessary to match the 5 m x 5 m resolution, with the same extent. A coarse simplification was done regarding the No Landslide Zones - NLZ, which were fixed to be the areas with slopes were less tan 20° or greater than 70°, with a 40 pixel sieve, meaning areas with less than 40 pixels were discarded as NLZ. From the Landslide inventory a Landslide Zone - LZ was defined as all of the areas were at least one landslide event had occourred. The LZ were substracted from the presumed NLZ, obtaining a NLZ-LZ rasterized layer with Hazard Areas on LZ and no Hazard Areas on the NLZ. A 70% random selection of polygons was used for the model training, and the other 30% ramained for validation. A random cloud of points was generated in between the NLZ-LZ layer on both training and testing sets, and their correspondant values from all the other raster layers were sampled. The model received as input all the raster layers mentioned above, plus the sampled testing and training points, generating a susceptability map for the given area, wich was then compared to the 30% of sampled points left for validation.

Input Layers

DTM

The Digital Terrain Model is a raster layer geospatial feature that provides gridded information of the Earth surface height, excluding vegetation and built features. For this case, a DTM of 5mx5m pixel resolution was used, and several layers where computed trough it’s derivatives like Slope, Aspect and Curvature.

SLOPE

calculated by comparing a certain point within a raster to that point's surrounding neighbor’s height values, describing its steepness, incline, or grade. A higher slope value indicates a steeper incline.

Aspect

Refers to the compass direction that a hillside or slope faces, often important in determining the amount of sunlight striking the land's surface, related to vegetation growth, soil moisture, among others.

Plan curvature

Refers to the second derivative along the direction of the slope, to understand whether the terrain curvature is concave or convex.

Profile curvature

Refers to the second derivative along the perpendicular direction of the slope, to understand whether the terrain curvature is concave or convex.

Land Use - DUSAF

Destinazione d'Uso dei Suoli Agricoli e Forestali – DUSAF, a raster layer geospatial feature containing land use destination, characteristics and vegetation cover of the Lombardy Region realized by the Italian ERSAF (Ente Regionale per i Servizi all'Agricoltura e Foreste).

NDVI

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index – NDVI, a widely used index derived by map algebra expression involving the visible red and near infrared (NIR) band. It is a simple, but effective index for quantifying green vegetation. The NDVI normalizes green leaf scattering in the NIR wavelength (Sentinel 2 - Band 8) and chlorophyll absorption in the Red wavelength (Sentinel 2 - Band 4).

Distance to Faults

A series of buffer distances from the corresponding lines depicting the Geological Faults of the working region. Distances were set to be 50, 100, 200, 500 m and over 500 m (9999 value), creating a raster layer of the different buffers for each line attribute.

Distance to Roads

A series of buffer distances from the corresponding lines depicting the Roads of the working region. Distances were set to be 50, 100, 200, 500 m and over 500 m (9999 value), creating a raster layer of the different buffers for each line attribute.

Distance to Rivers

A series of buffer distances from the corresponding lines depicting the Rivers of the working region. Distances were set to be 50, 100, 200, 500 m and over 500 m (9999 value), creating a raster layer of the different buffers for each line attribute.

LZ-NLZ

Landslide Zones - LZ and No Landslide Zones - NLZ, the first which were fixed to be the areas with slopes were less tan 20° or greater than 70°, and the second from the Landslide inventory a Landslide Zone - LZ was defined as all of the areas were at least one landslide event had occurred. The LZ were substracted from the presumed NLZ, obtaining a NLZ-LZ rasterized layer with Hazard Areas on LZ and no Hazard Areas on the NLZ.
